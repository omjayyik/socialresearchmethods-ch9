Chapter 9: Experimental Research

The Gold Standard for Testing Causal Relationships

The Logic of Experiments

Experimental research is the most effective method for testing a hypothesis about cause and effect. The researcher actively manipulates a situation to see what happens, allowing for powerful causal explanations.

The Anatomy of an Experiment

A true experiment is built from several key components. While not all studies have every part, the classical design includes all seven.

1. Treatment (Independent Variable)

The "cause" or the stimulus that the researcher manipulates.

2. Dependent Variable

The "effect" or the outcome that the researcher measures.

3. Pretest

A measurement of the dependent variable *before* the treatment is introduced.

4. Posttest

A measurement of the dependent variable *after* the treatment has been introduced.

5. Experimental Group

The group of participants that receives the treatment.

6. Control Group

The group of participants that does *not* receive the treatment, serving as a baseline for comparison.

7. Random Assignment

The most important element. This is the process of assigning participants to groups using a random method (like a coin flip). It ensures the groups are statistically identical before the experiment begins, eliminating many alternative explanations for any observed changes.

The Researcher's Dilemma: Validity

A good experiment must be valid, but there's a trade-off. Researchers must balance two types of validity, and strengthening one can often weaken the other.

This means we can confidently say that the treatment (IV) was the *only* thing that caused the change in the outcome (DV). A study has high internal validity when there are no confounding variables. Threats to internal validity include:

  • Selection Bias: Groups are not equivalent at the start of the study. (Solved by random assignment).
  • History: An external event occurs during the experiment that affects the results.
  • Maturation: Natural processes of growth or change in the participants over time affect the DV.
  • Testing: The pretest itself influences the results of the posttest.
  • Mortality: Participants drop out of the study, making the groups no longer equivalent.

üåç External Validity: Does it apply to the real world?

+

This is the ability to generalize the findings from the specific experimental setting to other people, places, and situations in the real world. Threats to external validity include:

  • Reactivity: Participants modify their behavior simply because they are aware they are being studied (also known as the Hawthorne Effect).
  • Artificial Setting: The highly controlled environment of a lab experiment may not reflect real-world conditions.
  • Unrepresentative Sample: The participants in the experiment (e.g., college students) may not be representative of the broader population.

A Typology of Designs

Experimental designs vary in their rigor and ability to demonstrate causality. We can group them into three main categories.

Weak: Pre-Experimental

Lack random assignment and a control group, making them poor for causal inference. They are often used in exploratory studies.
Example: One-Shot Case Study (A group gets a treatment, then a posttest is given).

Good: Quasi-Experimental

These designs are stronger because they have more controls, but they still lack random assignment. They are often used in real-world settings where random assignment isn't possible.
Example: Nonequivalent Control Group (Two existing groups are used; one gets the treatment, and both are pre- and post-tested).

Strongest: Classical Design

The "gold standard." It includes random assignment, a pretest, a posttest, an experimental group, and a control group. This design provides the strongest evidence for causality by eliminating most threats to internal validity.
